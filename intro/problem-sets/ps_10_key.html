Problem Set 10

Data Analysis: Education and Ethnicity in LA

Researchers in demography were interested in capturing the relationship between the ethnic make up of neighborhoods and the level of education. To gather data they took a random sample of 100 neighborhoods and recorded the proportion of residents in each that identified as hispanic and the proportion of residents that had graduated from college. The data can be loaded with the following command:

LA <- read.csv("http://andrewpbray.github.io/data/LAed.csv")
  1. Plot the relationship between ethnicity and education, keeping in mind the norm of putting the independent variable on the x-axis. Please describe the structure in the plot as well as any unusual observations.
library(ggplot2)
qplot(x = hispanic, y = education, data = LA, geom = "point")

There is a fairly linear, negative, moderately strong relationship between proportion of hispanic residents in a neighborhood and the college graduation rate. There are several notable outliers, including neighborhoods with graduation rates of 1 and another with a graduation rates of zero and a hispanic propotion also of zero.

  1. Fit a linear model to this scatterplot. Have you found a predictor that is statistically significant? In your regression output, what do you look for as evidence and how do you interpret it?
m1 <- lm(education ~ hispanic, data = LA)

  1. Was your inference conclusion based on a valid model? Please use plots to assess the conditions for regression.
qplot(x = .fitted, y = .stdresid, data = m1)

qplot(sample = .stdresid, data = m1) + geom_abline()

The residuals in the residual plot are consistently centered at zero, so the assumption of a linear trend seems reasonable. The question of constant variance is somewhat questionable as there is evidence of a slight increase in the variance as the fitted values increase. In terms of if these errors and their observations can be considered independent of one another, that is called into question by the description of how this data was collected. These constitutes a sample of 100 neighborhoods in LA, which may well be more than 10% of the total number of neighborhood, which would create some dependency between the observations in this sample. In the qqplot, the points generally do follow a line (with the exception of some strong outliers), which indicates the assumption of normality is reasonable.
